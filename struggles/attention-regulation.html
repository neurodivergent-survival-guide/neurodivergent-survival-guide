What This Feels Like

Attention regulationThe brain's ability to direct and sustain focus where you want it, when you want it issues often feel like having a spotlight that either shines too intensely in one direction or bounces around uncontrollably. Many people describe their attention as either completely absorbed by something interesting or impossible to focus on anything at all - with very little middle ground between these extremes.

You might find yourself reading the same paragraph five times without absorbing any information, while later that day becoming so engrossed in a video or project that hours pass without notice. During boring tasks, your mind might wander constantly to more interesting thoughts, making it nearly impossible to concentrate on what's in front of you.

Many people experience this as having "too much" attention rather than too little - your brain takes in everything around you, making it difficult to filter out distractions and focus on what's supposed to be important. This can feel exhausting, like your mental energyThe cognitive resources available for thinking, processing, and maintaining focus is being pulled in multiple directions simultaneously.

What Can Help You Through the Next 5 Minutes

When your attention feels scattered or stuck right now, these strategies can help regain some control:

  • Use the 2-minute ruleCommitting to focus for just 2 minutes, which often leads to longer periods of sustained attention: Set a timer for 2 minutes and commit to focusing only on one thing until it goes off. Often this creates enough momentum to continue.
  • Try environmental manipulationChanging your physical surroundings to support better attention and focus: Move to a different room, put on different music, or change your lighting. Your attention often responds to environmental shifts.
  • Use attention anchorsSpecific sensory inputs like background music, fidget tools, or physical positions that help maintain focus: Background music, a fidget toySmall objects that provide sensory input to help maintain attention and reduce restlessness, or specific seating can provide just enough stimulation to help regulate attention without being distracting.

  • Externalize distractions: Write down intrusive thoughts or interesting ideas so your brain doesn't have to hold onto them, freeing up working memoryThe mental workspace that temporarily holds and manipulates information during thinking tasks for the task at hand.
  • Use attention cuesExternal signals that remind your brain what to focus on: visual reminders, verbal self-prompts, or physical positioning: Say out loud what you're trying to focus on, or point to specific text while reading to help direct your attention.

Emergency attention rescue: If completely scattered, try naming 5 things you can see, 4 things you can touch, 3 things you can hear.
