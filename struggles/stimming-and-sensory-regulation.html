What Can Help You Through the Next 5 Minutes

When you need to regulate right now but are in a situation where stimming freely feels difficult:

  • Use covert stimsSubtle or hidden self-regulating behaviors that are less noticeable to others that work in your current environment: Press your fingertips together firmly, tense and release muscle groups, roll something small between your fingers, or trace patterns on your leg under a table. These provide proprioceptive inputSensory information from your muscles and joints about body position and movement without drawing attention.
  • Find acceptable movement within social constraints: Bouncing your foot, shifting in your chair, stretching, or walking to get water are often socially acceptable ways to get movement input when you can't engage in more obvious stimming.
  • Use breathing as regulation: Deep breathing provides interoceptive inputSensory information from inside your body, including awareness of breathing, heart rate, and internal states and can be done anywhere. Try breathing in for 4 counts, holding for 4, and out for 6 to activate your parasympathetic nervous systemThe part of your nervous system responsible for rest, relaxation, and recovery.
  • Seek appropriate sensory input: If you need deep pressure, press your back firmly against your chair or cross your arms and squeeze. For vestibular inputSensory information related to balance and movement, gentle head movements or shifting your weight can help when larger movements aren't possible.
  • Use objects designed for regulation: Fidget toys, stress balls, textured jewelry, or even a pen can provide sensory input. Many people find that having something in their hands helps them concentrate and feel more settled.
  • Take strategic breaks: If possible, step outside, visit the bathroom, or find a private space where you can move freely for a few minutes. Even 2-3 minutes of unrestricted movement can help reset your nervous system.
  • Practice self-advocacy when safe: If you're with understanding people, you can simply say "I need to move to concentrate" or "This helps me focus." Many people are more accepting when they understand the purpose.
  • Focus on regulation over appearance: Your ability to function is more important than appearing perfectly still or controlled. Find the least disruptive way to meet your sensory needs rather than suppressing them entirely.

Emergency regulation toolkit: Five quick covert stimsSubtle or hidden self-regulating behaviors that are less noticeable to others you can use anywhere - press palms together firmly, roll your shoulders, clench and release your jaw, trace small circles with your thumb, or shift your weight from foot to foot.

What Are Some Healthy Long-Term Solutions

Building sustainable systems for meeting your sensory regulationHow your nervous system processes and responds to sensory information to maintain optimal functioning needs across different environments:

  • Identify your specific sensory profile: Learn which types of input help you regulate (deep pressure, movement, textures, sounds) and which tend to overwhelm you. Understanding your patterns helps you seek appropriate input proactively rather than reactively.
  • Build a collection of regulation tools for different contexts: Develop stim kitsCollections of objects and tools that provide sensory input for regulation for work, social situations, and home. Include items like textured objects, fidget tools, weighted lap pads, or noise-canceling headphones.
  • Create sensory-friendly spaces in your environment: Designate areas where you can stim freely - your bedroom, office space, or even just a comfortable chair. Having reliable access to unrestricted movement helps prevent regulation deficits.
  • Educate important people in your life: Help family, friends, and colleagues understand that your stimmingSelf-stimulating behaviors like hand flapping, rocking, or fidgeting that help regulate emotions and sensory input serves a function.   • Develop sensory dietsPlanned activities throughout the day that provide the sensory input your nervous system needs and preventive regulation: Include regular movement, deep pressure activities, or other sensory input throughout your day to maintain optimal arousal levelsYour nervous system's state of alertness and activation rather than waiting until you're dysregulated.
  • Practice context-switching between regulation strategies: Learn which environments require more discrete stimming and which allow for more obvious movement. This helps you plan and adapt rather than feeling caught off-guard.
  • Build sensory regulation into your routines: Include movement breaks, deep pressure activities, or other regulatory input as part of your regular schedule. This makes regulation feel normal and necessary rather than disruptive.
  • Find movement-friendly activities and communities: Seek out environments where movement is expected or welcomed - dance, martial arts, fidget-friendly workspaces, or neurodivergent communitiesGroups of people with various neurological differences who understand and accept stimming that understand stimming.

  • Build relationships with other autistic people: Connecting with others who understand stimming can provide both social acceptance and practical strategies for regulation in different situations.

You're Not Imagining This

Your need for stimmingSelf-stimulating behaviors like hand flapping, rocking, or fidgeting that help regulate emotions and sensory input and specific sensory input is real, valid, and neurologically based. These aren't "bad habits" or signs of anxiety that need to be eliminated - they're functional behaviors that help your nervous system operate optimally.

The judgment you've received about your repetitive behaviorsActions you repeat that help you self-regulate, focus, or manage emotions reflects social misunderstanding rather than problems with your regulation strategies. Many successful autistic adults continue to stim throughout their lives and find ways to incorporate sensory regulation into their personal and professional environments.

Your sensory processing differencesHow your nervous system receives and interprets sensory information differently than typical are part of your neurological makeup, not character flaws or immaturity. The same nervous system that might need more movement or pressure than others often brings strengths like attention to detail, pattern recognition, or deep focus abilities.

It's completely understandable to feel frustrated by the conflict between your regulation needs and social expectations. You shouldn't have to choose between feeling comfortable in your body and being accepted by others, though navigating this balance is a real challenge many autistic people face daily.

The exhaustion that comes from suppressing natural self-regulation behaviorsActions that help calm and regulate your emotional and sensory state is genuine and can contribute to autistic burnoutExhaustion and functional decline from sustained efforts to appear neurotypical. Your body and brain function better when allowed to regulate naturally, and seeking environments and relationships that accommodate this is self-advocacy, not self-indulgence.

Remember: Your sensory and movement needs are valid neurological requirements, not preferences or habits. While learning to adapt to different social contexts can be helpful, the goal is accommodation and understanding, not elimination of your natural regulation strategies.
