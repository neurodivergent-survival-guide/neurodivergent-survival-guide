What is Autism?

Autism is a neurological difference that affects how people process social information, communicate, and experience sensory input. It's present from birth and influences how someone's brain develops and functions throughout their life.

Rather than being a single condition, autism exists on a spectrum—meaning it shows up differently in different people. Some autistic people might struggle significantly with daily tasks, while others live independently and may not be identified until adulthood. What unites all autistic people are differences in social communicationHow people share information, express needs, and connect with others through verbal and non-verbal means, sensory processingHow the nervous system receives and responds to sensory information from the environment, and patterns of behavior and interests.

These differences aren't inherently good or bad—they're simply variations in how human brains can work. The challenges many autistic people experience often come from living in environments designed for neurotypicalPeople whose brain development and functioning aligns with what society considers "typical" or "normal" brains rather than from autism itself.

Understanding Your Relationship with Autism

Whether you're recently discovering you might be autistic, have been formally diagnosed, or are simply trying to understand patterns in your life, reflection can help you better understand your experiences:

  • Do social interactions often leave you feeling drained, even when you enjoyed them?
  • Have you always felt like you were "performing" or "masking" in social situations?
  • Do you have intense interests that others might consider unusual or excessive?
  • Are you particularly sensitive to certain sounds, textures, lights, or other sensory experiences?
  • Do you prefer predictable routines and find unexpected changes distressing?
  • Have you been told you're "too direct" or that you miss social cues?
  • Do you find it difficult to understand what others are thinking or feeling without explicit communication?

Important Note

These reflection questions are for self-understanding only and are not diagnostic tools. Many experiences overlap between autism and other conditions, and only qualified professionals can provide clinical diagnoses.

When to Consider Professional Support

While understanding yourself better is valuable regardless of formal diagnosis, there are times when professional support becomes important:

Consider evaluation if:

  • Daily functioning is significantly impacted
  • You're experiencing depression or anxiety related to social struggles
  • You need workplace or school accommodations
  • Masking is causing severe burnout
  • You want clarity for personal understanding

Seek immediate help if:

  • You're having thoughts of self-harm
  • You're unable to care for basic needs
  • You're experiencing frequent meltdowns that affect your safety
  • Sensory issues are preventing you from leaving home
  • Social anxiety is causing complete isolation

Finding the right professional: Look for psychologists or psychiatrists who specifically mention autism in adults, neurodivergence, or have experience with late diagnoses. Many professionals still use outdated criteria that miss how autism presents in adults, particularly women and people assigned female at birth.

Remember: Autism is not something that needs to be "fixed" or "cured." It's a fundamental difference in how your brain works. The goal is understanding yourself better so you can navigate a world not designed for your type of brain, while honoring and protecting what makes you authentically you.
