What is ADHD?

ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder) involves brain differences in how attention is regulated, impulses are controlled, and daily tasks are managed. These differences create predictable friction with environments designed for neurotypical brains, particularly in schools and workplaces that emphasize sustained focus and traditional organizational methods.

ADHD isn't about lacking attention - it's about having a different type of attention that works best with interesting, challenging, urgent, or novel activities. Many people with ADHD can hyperfocus intensely on engaging tasks while struggling to start routine or boring ones.

Reframing ADHD

Traditional descriptions focus on what ADHD brains "can't do" compared to neurotypical expectations. A more helpful framework recognizes ADHD as an interest-based nervous systemA brain that functions optimally when tasks are interesting, challenging, urgent, or novel rather than an importance-based one.

This means your brain prioritizes what's engaging over what's important, which can create challenges in structured environments but also leads to incredible creativity, problem-solving abilities, and passion-driven achievements.

Attention Regulation Differences

Difficulty controlling where your focus goes, not a lack of attention. Many people with ADHD describe having "too much" attention that's hard to direct.

  • Hyperfocus on interesting activities
  • Difficulty sustaining focus on routine tasks
  • Easy distractibility by internal thoughts or external stimuli
  • Attention that shifts rapidly between topics

Executive Function Differences

Challenges with the brain's "management system" - the skills that help you plan, organize, and complete tasks.

  • Difficulty starting tasks (initiation paralysis)
  • Trouble with time awareness and planning
  • Working memory challenges
  • Organization and follow-through struggles

Hyperactivity and Impulsivity

Need for movement and tendency to act quickly without fully considering consequences. This can look different in adults compared to children.

  • Physical restlessness or fidgeting
  • Interrupting or blurting things out
  • Difficulty waiting or impatience
  • Quick decision-making that sometimes backfires

Emotional Intensity

Strong emotional responses and sensitivity to rejection or criticism. Emotions often feel more intense and change more quickly than for neurotypical people.

  • Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD)
  • Emotional ups and downs
  • Difficulty regulating emotional responses
  • High empathy and emotional attunement to others

Common Co-Occurring Conditions

ADHD frequently occurs alongside other neurodivergent conditions and mental health differences. Understanding these connections can help you get comprehensive support.

Frequently co-occurring: Autism (overlap in executive function and sensory differences), anxiety (often from years of struggling in neurotypical environments), depression (particularly from RSDRejection Sensitive Dysphoria - intense emotional pain from perceived rejection or criticism and chronic overwhelm), learning differences (dyslexia, dyscalculia), and sleep disorders.

Individual variation: ADHD presents differently across gender, age, and life circumstances. Many people, particularly women and girls, are diagnosed later in life after years of maskingCamouflaging natural behaviors to appear more neurotypical their differences.
