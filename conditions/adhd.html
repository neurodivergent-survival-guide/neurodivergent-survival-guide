What is ADHD?

ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder) involves brain differences in how attentionThe brain's ability to focus cognitive resources on specific information while filtering out distractions is regulated, impulsesQuick urges to act or respond before fully considering consequences are controlled, and daily tasks are managed. These differences create predictable friction with environments designed for neurotypicalPeople whose brains develop and function in ways that align with societal expectations and norms brains, particularly in schools and workplaces that emphasize sustained focus and traditional organizational methods.

ADHD isn't about lacking attention - it's about having a different type of attention that works best with interesting, challenging, urgent, or novel activities. Many people with ADHD can hyperfocusIntense, sustained concentration on a task of interest, often to the exclusion of everything else including basic needs intensely on engaging tasks while struggling to start routine or boring ones.

Reframing ADHD

Traditional descriptions focus on what ADHD brains "can't do" compared to neurotypicalPeople whose brains develop and function in ways that align with societal expectations and norms expectations. A more helpful framework recognizes ADHD as an interest-based nervous systemA brain that functions optimally when tasks are interesting, challenging, urgent, or novel, rather than simply important rather than an importance-based one.

This means your brain prioritizes what's engaging over what's important, which can create challenges in structured environments but also leads to incredible creativity, problem-solving abilities, and passion-driven achievements.

Attention RegulationThe brain's ability to direct and sustain focus where you want it, when you want it Differences

Difficulty controlling where your focus goes, not a lack of attention. Many people with ADHD describe having "too much" attention that's hard to direct.

  • HyperfocusIntense, sustained concentration on a task of interest, often to the exclusion of everything else including basic needs on interesting activities
  • Difficulty sustaining focus on routine tasks
  • Easy distractibility by internal thoughts or external stimuliEnvironmental inputs like sounds, lights, movements, or internal thoughts that capture attention
  • Attention that shifts rapidly between topics

Executive FunctionThe brain's management system that handles planning, organization, time management, task initiation, and follow-through Differences

Challenges with the brain's "management system" - the skills that help you plan, organize, and complete tasks.

  • Difficulty starting tasks (initiation paralysisKnowing what needs to be done but being unable to begin, despite wanting to)
  • Trouble with time awarenessUnderstanding how much time has passed, how long tasks will take, or managing schedules and planning
  • Working memoryThe brain's ability to hold and manipulate information temporarily while completing a task challenges
  • Organization and follow-through struggles

Hyperactivity and Impulsivity

Need for movement and tendency to act quickly without fully considering consequences. This can look different in adults compared to children.

  • Physical restlessness or fidgetingSmall repetitive movements that help regulate attention and energy levels
  • Interrupting or blurting things out
  • Difficulty waiting or impatience
  • Quick decision-making that sometimes backfires

Emotional Intensity

Strong emotional responses and sensitivity to rejection or criticism. Emotions often feel more intense and change more quickly than for neurotypicalPeople whose brains develop and function in ways that align with societal expectations and norms people.

  • Rejection Sensitive DysphoriaIntense emotional pain triggered by perceived rejection, criticism, or failure to meet expectations (RSD)
  • Emotional ups and downs
  • Difficulty regulating emotional responses
  • High empathyThe ability to understand and share the feelings of others and emotional attunement to others

Common Co-Occurring Conditions

ADHD frequently occurs alongside other neurodivergentBrain differences that create distinct patterns of thinking, processing, and behavior conditions and mental health differences. Understanding these connections can help you get comprehensive support.

Frequently co-occurring: Autism (overlap in executive functionThe brain's management system that handles planning, organization, time management, task initiation, and follow-through and sensory differencesVariations in how the brain processes sensory information like sounds, textures, lights, and movement), anxiety (often from years of struggling in neurotypicalPeople whose brains develop and function in ways that align with societal expectations and norms environments), depression (particularly from RSDRejection Sensitive Dysphoria - intense emotional pain triggered by perceived rejection, criticism, or failure to meet expectations and chronic overwhelm), learning differences (dyslexia, dyscalculia), and sleep disorders.

Individual variation: ADHD presents differently across gender, age, and life circumstances. Many people, particularly women and girls, are diagnosed later in life after years of maskingCamouflaging natural neurodivergent behaviors and responses to appear more neurotypical their differences.
