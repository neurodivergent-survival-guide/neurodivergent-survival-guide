Akathisia

When your body won't let you be still

Akathisia is a movement disorder that creates an overwhelming sense of inner restlessness and an irresistible urge to move. Unlike typical restlessness, this feels like your body is fighting against stillness at a cellular level.

This condition is most commonly caused by medications, particularly antipsychotics, but can also result from anti-nausea medications, antidepressants, or medication withdrawal.

Overwhelming Inner Restlessness

Medication Side Effect Movement Disorder

What this feels like:

An intense, uncomfortable feeling that you need to move or escape your own body. It's not anxiety - it's a physical compulsion that feels like electricity under your skin. You might feel like you're going to "jump out of your skin" or that sitting still is physically painful.

Why this might be happening:

Akathisia occurs when medications block dopamine receptors in the brain areas that control movement. This creates an imbalance that manifests as an irresistible urge to move. It's not psychological - it's a real neurological side effect.

What can help you through the next 5 minutes:

• Allow yourself to pace or rock - fighting the urge makes it worse

• Try gentle stretching or walking if possible

• Use deep breathing while moving (don't force stillness)

• Cool compress on your neck or face while pacing

• Remind yourself this is medication-related, not anxiety

What are some healthy long-term solutions:

• Contact your prescribing doctor immediately - this is a medical issue requiring professional intervention

• Ask about dose reduction, medication switching, or anticholinergic medications

• Keep a movement diary to track patterns and severity

• Consider propranolol or other akathisia treatments

• Advocate for yourself - this side effect is often dismissed but is very real

When should I consider immediate medical intervention:

• If the restlessness is severe and interfering with sleep, eating, or basic functioning

• If you're having thoughts of self-harm due to the distress

• If it developed recently after starting or changing a medication

• If over-the-counter movement or anxiety techniques provide no relief

You're not imagining this:

Akathisia is a recognized medical condition, not "just anxiety" or restlessness. Many people experience this side effect, and it requires medical attention. Your distress is valid, and there are treatments available. Don't let anyone minimize what you're experiencing.

Inability to Sit Still

Physical Symptom Medication Related

What this feels like:

Your body literally won't let you sit still. Even when exhausted, you feel compelled to pace, rock, shift positions constantly, or move your legs. Trying to force stillness feels torturous and increases the uncomfortable sensation.

Why this might be happening:

The medication is interfering with your brain's movement control systems. Unlike ADHD hyperactivity, this feels compulsive rather than energetic. Your brain is sending constant "move now" signals that you can't ignore.

What can help you through the next 5 minutes:

• Give in to the movement urge - pacing is better than forcing stillness

• Try rocking in place if you must stay seated

• Fidget toys or stress balls for hand movement

• Stand and stretch during conversations or activities

• Use movement as a coping tool, not something to fight

What are some healthy long-term solutions:

• Work with your doctor to adjust medication timing or dosing

• Request a different medication in the same class

• Consider adjunct treatments like pyridoxine or beta-blockers

• Modify your environment to allow for movement (standing desk, walking meetings)

• Educate family/employers about this medical condition

When should I consider immediate medical intervention:

• If you can't sit for necessary activities (driving, work, medical appointments)

• If the movement compulsion is getting stronger over time

• If you're injuring yourself from constant movement

• If sleep is severely disrupted by the need to move

You're not imagining this:

This compulsive movement is a real side effect that many people experience. It's not hyperactivity, anxiety, or lack of self-control. Medical professionals should take this seriously and work with you to find solutions that allow you to get the mental health treatment you need without unbearable side effects.

Disrupted Daily Functioning

Functional Impact Social Difficulty

What this feels like:

Simple activities become impossible. You can't sit through meals, meetings, movies, or conversations. People might think you're rude or anxious, but you literally cannot stay still. Social situations become awkward when you need to constantly move.

Why this might be happening:

The medication is making normal sitting/stillness physically uncomfortable. Your brain's movement control is disrupted, making activities that require stillness feel torturous rather than restful.

What can help you through the next 5 minutes:

• Explain to others that you have a medical condition requiring movement

• Stand during conversations when possible

• Take frequent "stretch breaks" during activities

• Choose aisle seats or positions where you can move more freely

• Use phone calls for meetings when you need to pace

What are some healthy long-term solutions:

• Work with your doctor to address the underlying medication issue

• Request accommodations at work/school (standing desk, movement breaks)

• Develop a brief explanation for social situations

• Find activities that naturally involve movement

• Connect with others who understand medication side effects

When should I consider immediate medical intervention:

• If you're unable to work or attend essential activities

• If relationships are suffering due to misunderstanding of your condition

• If the functional impairment is worse than your original mental health condition

• If driving becomes unsafe due to inability to sit still

You're not imagining this:

Your functional difficulties are directly related to a medication side effect, not lack of willpower or social skills. Many people face this exact challenge. You deserve understanding and appropriate medical management that allows you to function in daily life.

Medication Dilemma

Treatment Balance Medical Decision

What this feels like:

You're caught between needing psychiatric medication for your mental health and suffering from unbearable movement side effects. You might feel like you have to choose between mental stability and physical comfort, which feels impossible and unfair.

Why this might be happening:

Many effective psychiatric medications can cause movement disorders. This creates a genuine treatment dilemma where the cure feels worse than the disease. Healthcare providers sometimes underestimate how distressing these side effects can be.

What can help you through the next 5 minutes:

• Remind yourself that this is a solvable medical problem

• Document your side effects to discuss with your doctor

• Remember that many medication options exist

• Connect with support groups who understand this dilemma

• Know that you don't have to suffer indefinitely

What are some healthy long-term solutions:

• Work with a psychiatrist experienced in managing movement side effects

• Explore alternative medications in different classes

• Consider adjunct treatments to manage akathisia while maintaining mental health benefits

• Get second opinions if your current provider dismisses your concerns

• Learn about your rights as a patient to refuse treatments with intolerable side effects

When should I consider immediate medical intervention:

• If your current provider refuses to address the side effects

• If you're considering stopping necessary medication due to side effects

• If you're experiencing both mental health crisis and severe movement side effects

• If you need emergency psychiatric care but are avoiding it due to fear of medication side effects

You're not imagining this:

You have the right to mental health treatment that doesn't cause unbearable physical side effects. A good psychiatrist will work with you to find solutions that address both your mental health needs and your quality of life. Don't let anyone convince you that severe side effects are "just part of treatment."
